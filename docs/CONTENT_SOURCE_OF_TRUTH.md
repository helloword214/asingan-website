# Content Source of Truth

## Purpose

This document defines how public-facing website content for the Asingan Fire Station website should read.

It exists to keep the site:

- clear
- concise
- story-led
- reader-friendly
- consistent across pages

This is a `public content` guide, not a research log and not a source-verification file.

## Scope

Use this document for:

- page headlines
- hero copy
- section intros
- short body copy
- captions
- cards
- calls to action
- page-level narrative flow

Do not use this document for:

- source tracking
- verification notes
- research uncertainty logs
- internal implementation commentary
- raw data wording

Those belong in internal docs, source notes, or structured data layers instead.

Pages currently found in the app routes:

- `Home`

- `About / Station Profile`

## Core Rule

Public pages must sound like a real website for real readers.

They must not sound like:

- an internal build note
- a documentation draft
- a data export
- a source-verification memo
- a developer handoff

## Content Priorities

When writing or editing content, prioritize in this order:

1. clarity
2. reader interest
3. brevity
4. storytelling flow
5. consistency across pages
6. completeness only where needed

If a sentence is accurate but heavy, rewrite it.

If a sentence is informative but dull, tighten it.

If a sentence is useful only to the team and not to the reader, remove it from the public page.

## Voice and Tone

The site voice should feel:

- official
- warm
- steady
- readable
- confident
- respectful
- community-centered

The writing should not feel:

- stiff
- academic
- overly promotional
- dramatic for its own sake
- robotic
- too technical

## Theme Anchor

The site-wide public content theme is:

- `Asingan Fire Station`
- `Working Together for a Safer Asingan`
- `From emergency response and fire suppression to prevention and community preparedness, Asingan Fire Station continues to serve and support the safety of the municipality through dedicated public service.`

This is the core story anchor for the website.

Public-facing copy across pages should align with this theme by reinforcing:

- teamwork
- service
- response
- prevention
- preparedness
- safety of the municipality

This does not mean every page should repeat the same exact words.

It means every page should sound like part of the same story.

Use the theme like a narrative compass:

- `Home` states it most clearly
- `History` shows how the station's service story grew over time
- `Leadership` reflects stewardship, continuity, and responsibility
- `Organization` shows how teams and sections work together
- `Personnel` reflects the people behind the service
- `Assets` reflects the tools that support readiness and response

If a page headline, lede, section intro, or caption feels disconnected from this story, it should be revised.

## Reader Assumption

Assume the public reader:

- scans before reading deeply
- gives each section only a short chance
- wants to understand the point quickly
- loses patience when content repeats itself
- responds better to structure and rhythm than to long explanation

## Writing Style Rules

- Prefer short sentences over layered ones.
- Prefer strong nouns and verbs over filler phrases.
- Prefer one clear point per paragraph.
- Prefer natural phrasing over document-style phrasing.
- Prefer simple direct wording over institutional padding.

Good paragraph length:

- `1 to 3 sentences` for most section intros
- `2 to 4 sentences` for narrative body blocks

Avoid long uninterrupted blocks unless the page absolutely needs them.

## Concision Rules

- Cut repeated ideas even if the wording is different.
- Do not explain the same fact in the hero, section intro, and card body.
- Do not restate the page title in the first paragraph unless it adds meaning.
- Do not use two sentences where one sentence already works.
- Do not add throat-clearing lines just to introduce a section.

If a sentence can be removed without losing meaning, it should usually be removed.

## Storytelling Rule

Every page should feel guided.

That means the page should answer:

1. what this page is about
2. why it matters
3. what the reader should notice next

Storytelling should be present, but concise.

We are not trying to write essays.
We are trying to help the reader move through the page with interest and without friction.

## White Space Rule

White space is part of the content system.

That means:

- sections should have room to breathe
- not every area needs explanation text
- short blocks are better than packed walls of copy
- a clean stop is better than an extra paragraph

If a layout already communicates something visually, the copy should not over-explain it.

## Redundancy Rule

Each page should have a clear content role.

Do not repeat the same explanation across multiple pages unless the reader truly needs it there.

Examples:

- `Home` should introduce, not fully explain
- `History` should carry the longer historical narrative
- `Leadership` should focus on continuity of station leadership
- `Assets` should focus on response capability and support role
- `Personnel` should focus on people, roles, and service identity

If a block belongs more naturally to another page, shorten it or move it there.

## Public vs Internal Boundary

Public copy should not expose internal process language.

Keep these kinds of wording out of public pages:

- `currently encoded`
- `stored in the project files`
- `record set`
- `source notes`
- `public profile route`
- `this page now`
- `document-backed`
- `pending exact date confirmation`
- `not yet confirmed`
- `source not listed`

That language is acceptable in:

- source notes
- working docs
- internal admin tools
- data QA layers

It is not acceptable as normal visitor-facing copy.

## Wording Rules for Uncertain Information

If a detail is not strong enough for confident public wording:

- either leave it out
- or phrase it simply and neutrally

Do not burden the main reading flow with internal caution labels.

If a note must remain visible for accuracy, keep it short and low-friction.

## Headline Rules

Headlines should:

- say something meaningful
- avoid sounding generic
- stay easy to scan
- fit the page role

Avoid headlines that sound like placeholders or file summaries.

Bad direction:

- `Present-to-past record of station heads currently encoded in the project data.`

Better direction:

- `A look at the station's line of leadership over time.`

## Hero Rules

Each hero should contain:

- `1` strong headline
- `1` short supporting paragraph
- up to `2` clear actions

Hero support copy should answer:

- who the station is
- what it does
- why the page matters

It should not contain:

- internal process notes
- source disclaimers
- repeated page-summary filler

## Section Intro Rules

Most sections need only:

- one short label
- one useful heading
- one short setup line if necessary

If the cards or visuals already carry the meaning, the intro can be minimal.

## Card Copy Rules

Card copy should be tightly edited.

- Do not repeat the card title in the paragraph.
- Do not explain obvious visuals.
- Do not turn small cards into mini-essays.
- Keep captions and support text short.

If a card is already clear from its heading, the body should add only one new layer of meaning.

## CTA Rules

Calls to action should be:

- short
- concrete
- reader-facing

Prefer:

- `View apparatus`
- `Read the station history`
- `Meet the personnel`

Avoid vague or internal labels.

## Page Roles

### Home

Purpose:

- introduce the station
- show public value quickly
- guide readers into deeper pages

Home should feel:

- welcoming
- capable
- easy to scan

Home should not feel:

- overloaded with explanation
- like a full archive page
- like a project status board

### History

Purpose:

- tell the station's story across time
- show growth, continuity, and local grounding

History should emphasize:

- beginnings
- milestones
- transition
- permanence
- service to the community

History should not read like a legal digest or source list.

### Leadership

Purpose:

- show continuity of responsibility and stewardship

Leadership should feel:

- respectful
- orderly
- concise

The focus is the leadership line, not internal dataset language.

### Organization

Purpose:

- help readers understand how the station is structured to serve

Organization copy should explain:

- what each group or section handles
- how the structure supports operations

Avoid exposing raw source labels in the main reading flow when they are not needed by the public reader.

### Personnel

Purpose:

- present the people behind the station in a dignified, useful way

Personnel content should feel:

- human
- respectful
- structured

It should not read like a database screen.

### Assets

Purpose:

- show what supports station response and readiness

Assets copy should focus on:

- response role
- operational value
- context where meaningful

It should not feel like an inventory spreadsheet translated directly into the UI.

### Future Pages

When these are added, their content roles should be:

- `About / Station Profile`: station identity and public-service role
- `EMS and Rescue`: rescue and medical-response story
- `Services`: practical public-facing service overview
- `News and Stories`: fresh updates and human-centered station narratives
- `Gallery`: visual storytelling with minimal but useful captions
- `Contact / Emergency Information`: fastest-access public information on the site

## Editing Checklist

Before finalizing any page copy, ask:

1. does this sound public-facing
2. does this sound human
3. is the point clear in one quick read
4. is anything repeated
5. can a sentence be shortened
6. is the section carrying too much explanation
7. does the copy leave enough breathing room
8. does the wording help the reader keep going

If the answer to any of these is no, revise again.

## Working Standard

The best public content on this site should feel:

- easy to enter
- easy to continue
- easy to remember

It should guide the reader with confidence, not test their patience.
